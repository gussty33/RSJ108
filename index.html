How to Conduct and Interview

Assembly time: 30 minutes
Awesome rating: 90% awesome

Are you wondering how to conduct an in-person interview successfully and efficiently? Follow this step-by-step guide to make the interviewing process as smooth and as stress free as possible.

  • Things You'll Need

  • Notepad
  • Pen or pencil
  • Access to the internet
  • Recording device (optional but useful)
  • A firm handshake
  • Manners

  1. Research the person.

    You should know as much as possible about the person you're interviewing so you go into the interview feeling prepared and in control. Look up as much relevant information about the person as you can.

  2. Clarify your objective.

    Before you prepare your interview questions, you should understand your objective for interviewing the person. Is it simply to give readers more insight into that person's personal life, to discuss one aspect of that person's career, or to discuss that person's perspective on a certain political situation? Whatever your objective, your questions should help you meet your objectives. If you're very clear about your objective, you'll also be able to keep your interview more focused and to make sure you don't drift off-topic.

    Interview Notes.

  3. Prepare questions.

    You should prepare questions that are flexible, open-ended, and which all come equipped with at least two or three follow-up questions that you can ask depending on the answer to the original question.

  4. Make the person comfortable.

    This is the most important thing you can do before you officially give the interview. After you've introduced yourself, you can make the person comfortable with some small talk, some light jokes, and by making eye contact and using your hands to gesture or keeping them at your sides. Keep your body open and posed toward that person without invading his personal space.

  5. Ask your questions.

    Maintain eye contact as you ask the question and listen intently to the answer. Don't say, "My first question is..." or "My next question is..." Make the person feel like you're just having a natural conversation, not firing questions at him like a detective. Always start out with questions that understand who the person is, understanding what they want, understand what the challenges are they're facing, and what the path is they want to take so that you can help them achieve those goals.

  6. Thank the person.

    You should sincerely thank the person for taking the time to chat with you and for being so patient and answering your questions. Make sure your words and body language indicate that you really mean it and are truly grateful for the person's time and efforts. Don't become withdrawn the second the interview is over. Instead, continue to be warm and welcoming even after you've put your recorder or notebook away.

    Interview.
